The conversation about menstrual health at work has shifted. It is no longer just an advocacy talking point or a fringe HR discussion. A growing number of companies are moving from intention to action, putting real structures in place that make a genuine difference to the women who work for them. Here are five of the most practical and proven solutions being implemented right now.

**1. Free period products, everywhere**

The simplest place to start is also one of the most impactful. The <a href="https://www.periodpositiveworkplace.org" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Period Positive Workplace</a> programme, a UK initiative helping organisations become more menstruation-friendly, consistently identifies product access as a foundational step. Having period products available in all workplace toilets, not just some of them, removes a quiet but real source of stress for millions of women every month. <a href="https://workplace.totm.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener">TOTM</a>, a UK-based sustainable period care brand, offers a workplace scheme that delivers organic period products directly to offices, making the logistics straightforward for employers of any size. For women working in environments where leaving a workstation is difficult, or where the nearest shop is not nearby, this is not a small thing.

**2. Paid menstrual leave**

A smaller number of companies have gone further, introducing paid menstrual leave as a formal benefit. Zomato, the Indian food delivery company, made headlines in 2020 when it introduced ten days of paid period leave per year for all employees and delivery partners who menstruate. Larsen and Toubro, one of India's largest engineering conglomerates, followed with its own menstrual leave policy covering its female workforce. The debate around menstrual leave is not without complexity, and implementation varies significantly by culture and context. But what these companies demonstrate is that it is operationally possible. <a href="https://tech.co/news/companies-offer-menstrual-leave" target="_blank" rel="noopener">See which companies currently offer menstrual leave.</a>

**3. Manager training that changes culture**

Policy without culture change rarely sticks. <a href="https://www.bloodygoodemployers.com" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Bloody Good Employers</a>, a UK organisation founded specifically to help workplaces improve menstrual health support, offers training and resources designed to help managers have better conversations about periods at work. Their framework helps teams move from awkwardness and avoidance to practical, confident responses. The result is not just better support for individual women, but a broader cultural shift in which menstrual health becomes a normal part of how a workplace talks about wellbeing, rather than something managed in silence.

**4. Period-friendly certification**

Some organisations are formalising their commitment through external certification. <a href="https://menstruationfriendly.co.uk" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Menstruation Friendly UK</a> provides a recognised standard for employers who have put meaningful menstrual health support in place. Achieving the certification involves demonstrating real action across policy, product access, education and culture, rather than simply signing a pledge. For HR teams looking to benchmark progress or demonstrate commitment to candidates and staff, it offers a credible and structured framework to work toward.

**5. Clinical support and reproductive health benefits**

The most comprehensive approaches extend beyond periods into broader reproductive and hormonal health. <a href="https://www.fertifa.com/post/companies-that-offer-the-best-menopause-benefits" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Fertifa</a>, a UK employee benefits platform, enables companies to offer their workforce access to clinical support covering fertility, menopause, menstrual health and more. Companies including Monzo have used platforms like Fertifa to signal that reproductive health is a serious part of their benefits package, not an afterthought. For women managing conditions like PCOS, endometriosis or severe PMS alongside a demanding career, access to clinical guidance through their employer can be genuinely life-changing.

What all five of these approaches share is a recognition that supporting women during their periods is not about lowering the bar. It is about removing unnecessary obstacles so that women can clear the bar they have always been capable of clearing. The companies doing this well are not doing it out of charity. They are doing it because it works, and because it is the right thing to do.

The blueprint exists. The question now is who picks it up.

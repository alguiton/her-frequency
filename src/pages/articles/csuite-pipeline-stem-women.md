The pipeline problem in STEM is not really a pipeline problem. It is a retention problem. And the data has been telling us this for years.

Women enter science, technology, engineering and mathematics at broadly comparable rates to men in many developed economies. The gap that persists — and it persists stubbornly — is not at the point of entry. It is in the years that follow. Women leave STEM careers at significantly higher rates than their male peers, and they tend to leave at predictable career stages: in their late twenties and early thirties, often coinciding with early career consolidation, the beginning of family formation, and the first encounter with cultures that were not designed with them in mind.

UNESCO data shows that women represent 33% of researchers globally, a figure that has barely shifted in a decade. In the UK, WISE Campaign data puts women at 29% of the core STEM workforce. In energy specifically, the IEA's Women in Energy report places female representation in energy sector employment at around 22%. These numbers are not moving fast enough, and the reason is not a shortage of qualified women. It is a shortage of conditions in which qualified women choose to stay.

The research on why women leave STEM is consistent across sectors and geographies. It points to three primary drivers: culture, flexibility, and health support — or rather, the absence of all three.

Culture in many STEM organisations still rewards a particular model of professional presence: long hours, high visibility, linear career progression, and a degree of social conformity that tends to suit those who built the culture in the first place. Women — and particularly women from underrepresented backgrounds — frequently describe feeling invisible, unsupported, or required to perform a version of professionalism that does not fit. When the culture does not reflect you, staying requires a constant expenditure of energy that is simply not required of those who fit the default.

Flexibility remains unequally distributed. Despite widespread adoption of hybrid working policies post-pandemic, CIPD research consistently shows that women are less likely than men to have genuine schedule flexibility — the kind that allows them to manage medical appointments, family responsibilities, or a health condition like endometriosis or PCOS while maintaining a demanding career. The policy exists. The practice often does not.

Health support is the piece that organisations are only beginning to reckon with. Women in STEM disproportionately carry the burden of managing conditions that affect their working lives — menstrual health conditions, perimenopausal symptoms, pregnancy loss, fertility treatment — in environments that treat these as private matters to be dealt with outside of working hours. The cost is significant. Research increasingly links inadequate menstrual health support to higher rates of presenteeism, sick leave and ultimately attrition among women in professional roles.

The business case for addressing this is not complicated. Recruiting a senior engineer or scientist costs, on average, between 50% and 200% of their annual salary. Retaining them costs a fraction of that — if the investment is made in the right places. Organisations that have introduced flexible scheduling, menstrual health policies, and genuine manager training on these issues report measurably higher retention rates among women, particularly at the mid-career stage.

None of this requires treating women as fragile. It requires treating women as professionals whose needs have simply not been built into the system yet — and fixing that, pragmatically and without drama.

The women who leave STEM are not leaving because they lack ambition or capability. They are leaving because the calculation — of effort required to stay, versus reward received — tips in the wrong direction. Tip it back, and they will stay. The evidence is clear. The question is whether organisations are willing to act on it.

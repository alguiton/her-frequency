It is time to look clearly at what the science actually says — and what it does not.

There is a version of the conversation about women, hormones and work that has always made people uncomfortable. The fear is that acknowledging biology leads to assumptions: that women are somehow less reliable, less consistent, less capable than their male counterparts. This concern is understandable. And it is worth addressing directly, because the evidence tells a more nuanced and ultimately more empowering story.

The research on menstrual cycles and cognitive performance does not show that women are less productive. What it shows is that women experience variation — and that this variation, when understood and accommodated, can be a source of strength rather than a liability.

Multiple studies have examined cognitive function across the menstrual cycle. The picture that emerges is of fluctuation rather than deficit. During the follicular phase, when oestrogen is rising, women tend to show stronger verbal fluency, faster information processing and higher self-reported confidence. During the luteal phase, there is often a shift toward greater attention to detail, stronger risk-awareness and deeper concentration on single tasks. These are not lesser qualities. They are different qualities — and in a well-designed working environment, they are all valuable.

The McKinsey *Women in the Workplace* report, published annually, consistently shows that women perform on a par with — and in many areas outperform — their male peers when measured by outcomes. The gender gap in promotions, pay and seniority that persists across industries is not explained by performance differences. It is explained by structural inequity: who gets sponsored, who is seen, who is given stretch opportunities, and who carries the invisible load of domestic and care responsibilities outside of work.

Framing menstrual cycles as a performance problem misunderstands the data entirely. The data shows that cycles affect the *experience* of work — comfort, energy, confidence — not fundamental capability. The women who report the most negative impact from their cycles at work are overwhelmingly in environments with rigid scheduling, no flexibility, high presenteeism pressure and no room to manage their own time. The solution is not to change the women. It is to change the environment.

This matters because the stakes are real. Seven in ten women report that their period negatively affects them at work. Ninety per cent experience anxiety linked to menstruation in professional settings. These are not statistics about incapacity — they are statistics about unsupported need. When needs are met, performance is not diminished. It is protected.

For senior leaders: the most evidence-based position available to you is not that cycles are irrelevant to work, nor that they represent a liability. It is that cycles are a biological reality that workplaces have historically ignored, and that ignoring them has a measurable cost. Flexible working, access to wellbeing support, a culture that allows honest conversation about health — these are not accommodations to weakness. They are investments in the conditions under which all people perform best.

The science does not ask us to see women as limited by their biology. It asks us to see them as whole people, operating within systems that were built without them in mind — and to build better systems.

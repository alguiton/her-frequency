Every day, millions of women go to work while managing period symptoms. Cramps, fatigue, headaches, back pain, low mood. They sit in meetings, hit deadlines, lead presentations and answer emails, often without a word to anyone, because the culture around them has never made it easy to say anything at all.

This is not about asking for special treatment. It is about recognising that menstrual health is part of working life for a significant portion of the workforce, and that workplaces have a genuine opportunity to do better.

Menstrual symptoms vary widely from person to person. For some women, a period is a minor inconvenience that passes quickly. For others, the physical and emotional impact is significant enough to affect concentration, energy, confidence and comfort throughout the working day. Research from the CIPD shows that menstrual symptoms have a meaningful effect on many women's experience at work, yet the majority feel unable to tell their manager the real reason they are struggling. Instead, they use sick days, push through silently, or simply perform below the level they are capable of because the support was never there.

The good news is that the changes that make the biggest difference are often simple. Free period products in workplace toilets remove a small but genuine source of stress, particularly in environments where women cannot easily leave their desk or workstation. Clean, private bathroom facilities where women feel comfortable managing their symptoms with dignity matter more than most organisations realise. The ability to take a short extra break, adjust a deadline by a day, or work from a quieter space on a difficult morning can make the difference between a woman feeling managed and supported, or invisible and pressured.

Manager awareness is one of the most impactful levers available. A manager who understands that menstrual health can affect performance, and who responds to a team member's needs with practical flexibility rather than discomfort or dismissal, creates an environment where women do not have to choose between honesty and professionalism. This does not require managers to become medical experts. It requires them to listen well, ask practical questions and treat the conversation with the same matter-of-fact respect they would bring to any other health-related discussion.

Some organisations are going further, introducing menstrual health policies that sit alongside existing wellbeing frameworks. These policies make clear what support is available, how to access it and how conversations about menstrual health will be handled. They signal to every woman in the organisation that her experience is taken seriously and that she does not have to manage alone. The EIS guidance on menstrual health at work offers a useful starting point for employers who want to build something meaningful rather than tokenistic.

What tends to stop organisations from acting is not cost or complexity. It is discomfort with the topic itself. Menstruation remains one of the last genuinely taboo subjects in many professional environments, despite affecting roughly half the working population for a significant portion of their careers. That discomfort is understandable, but it is not a good enough reason to leave women unsupported.

The workplaces that get this right do not do it because it is politically fashionable. They do it because it works. When women feel supported, trust in the organisation grows. Retention improves. Presenteeism decreases. And women who might otherwise have spent their energy managing privately, in silence, get to spend that energy on their actual work instead.

Small changes, consistently made, add up to a culture where menstrual health is simply part of how a workplace thinks about its people. That is not an ambitious target. It is a basic one. And it is well within reach.
